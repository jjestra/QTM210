The Poisson distribution

The Poisson process models the number of events that occur during each of a series units.

In [1]:
event <- c(1,1,2,1,0,3)
mean(event)
1.33333333333333

The units are often time or space. For instance:

  • Number of costumers per minute calling a help center
  • Number of wars starting in a given year
  • Number of trees in an acre

The probability distribution of the Poisson gives the probability of observing kk events in a time period --given the length of the period and the average events per period--. Let the parameter represent the rate at which events occur (e.g., might be expressed as 2.5 events per minute), then:

PX(k)=P(X=k)=ekk!,k=0,1,2,PX(k)=P(X=k)=ekk!,k=0,1,2,

2. Waiting Time and the exponential distribution

The relevant question is to find out how long we have to wait for the next event to happen:

Theorem 4.2.3: Suppose a series of events satisfying the Poisson model are occurring at the rate of per unit time. Let the random variable YY denote the interval between consecutive events. Then YY has the exponential distribution

fY(y)=ey,y>0fY(y)=ey,y>0

As mentioned before, the exponential distribution predicts the amount of waiting time until the next event. For example the amount of time you need to wait until the bus arrives (arrival time). The exponential distribution takes the parameter as input. Intuitively, this parameter represents the proportion of times that the event occurs during a unit of time.
